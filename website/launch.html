About Project

Our project focuses on the detection of harmful algal blooms in ocean bodies through a user-friendly front-end interface. The process begins with users providing coordinates of the Region of Interest (ROI) they wish to monitor. Utilizing these coordinates, our system accesses data from MODIS and Sentinel satellites, as well as meteorological data from relevant regional websites via API calls.

Once the preprocessing is complete, the resulting data is structured into a coherent dataframe, facilitating streamlined analysis and interpretation. Subsequently, the prepared data is fed into our machine learning model, trained to identify the presence and extent of algal blooms within the designated ROI.

In the event of detected algal blooms or an increase in their severity, our system automatically triggers notifications to relevant oceanic authorities via email. This proactive approach aims to facilitate timely response measures, thereby mitigating potential environmental impacts and safeguarding marine ecosystems.

Feature Extraction from Satellite Imagery

To extract the features listed in your prompt from satellite imagery, we can employ various filtering mechanisms and image processing techniques:

  • Spatial Filtering: Convolutional filters, such as Gaussian filters, can be applied to enhance specific features.
  • Spectral Filtering: Techniques like NDVI and NDWI can highlight certain features or suppress noise.
  • Statistical Filtering: Computing statistical metrics across the image can provide valuable information about surface properties.
  • Feature Extraction: Specific features like temperature, sea level pressure, and chlorophyll concentration can be derived from the processed satellite imagery.
  • Integration and Analysis: Extracted features are integrated and analyzed using machine learning algorithms to predict algal bloom events and identify hotspot areas.

Data Used

Satellite Data Sources:

  • Sentinel-2A: Operated by the European Space Agency (ESA), Sentinel-2A is part of the Copernicus program. It provides high-resolution optical imagery with 13 spectral bands, ranging from visible to shortwave infrared.
  • MODIS (Moderate Resolution Imaging Spectroradiometer): Deployed on NASA's Terra and Aqua satellites, MODIS captures data in 36 spectral bands, including visible, near-infrared, and thermal infrared. It offers a wider swath width compared to Sentinel-2, albeit at coarser spatial resolution.
